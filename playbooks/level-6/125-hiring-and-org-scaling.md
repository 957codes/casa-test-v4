# Hiring and Org Scaling

When the binding constraint becomes people rather than product or demand, the
company has to build a hiring function before it adds headcount. This play installs
the first key hires, a scorecard for each role, a repeatable pipeline, structured
onboarding, and an org design that holds as the team grows. It is the ready target
the moment the founder's do-or-die constraint is hiring capacity.

## Procedure

1. Name the constraint precisely. Identify the two or three roles whose absence is
   capping output right now and rank them by the revenue or delivery they unblock.
   Hire against the bottleneck, not against a generic org chart.
2. Write a scorecard for each role before sourcing: the mission, the four to six
   measurable outcomes that define success in the first year, and the competencies
   that predict them. A role without a scorecard cannot be interviewed consistently
   or held accountable later.
3. Build the pipeline: sourcing channels, a structured interview loop mapped to the
   scorecard, a work sample or paid trial where the role allows, and a reference
   step. Define the bar and the decision rule (who decides, on what evidence) so
   hiring does not stall on the founder.
4. Design onboarding to first contribution: a 30/60/90 plan tied to the scorecard
   outcomes, the access and context a new hire needs in week one, and a named owner.
   Time to productivity is the metric that justifies the hire.
5. Set the org design one layer ahead: reporting lines, decision rights, and the
   spans and layers the next stage needs, so each hire fits a structure rather than
   forcing a reorg later. Record the open roles, scorecards, and structure in
   `hiring_plan`.

## Output

`hiring_plan`: the ranked open roles, a scorecard per role, the interview loop and
decision rule, the onboarding plan, and the org design for the next stage, written
to the company brain. Feeds the operating cadence and the scaling-stage build map.

## Rules

- Hire against the binding constraint, not a template org chart. The next hire is
  the one that unblocks the most output.
- No scorecard, no requisition. An unmeasurable role produces inconsistent
  interviews and an unmanageable hire.
- The founder sets the bar and the decision rule, then delegates the loop. A
  pipeline that routes every decision back to the founder does not scale.
- A hire is reversible but costly. Be slow to hire against a clear bar and fast to
  act when a hire is clearly wrong.

Each senior hire and each new layer passes a founder gate. The plan is built once
and revisited each stage, not rerun per role.
