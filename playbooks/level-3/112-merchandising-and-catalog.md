# Merchandising & Catalog

Merchandising is how the catalog is organized and presented so a shopper finds the right
product and reaches for a larger basket. It earns its place once the catalog is deep enough
that structure matters; for a single-product store it is overkill. The levers are catalog
taxonomy, the product detail page (PDP), collections, and bundles.

## Procedure

1. Structure the catalog: a clear category taxonomy, consistent attributes and filters, and
   a naming and tagging scheme that makes search and navigation predictable.
2. Merchandise the PDP: the hero image set, a benefit-led title and description, social
   proof, clear variant selection, and the cross-sell and upsell blocks.
3. Build collections: curated groupings (use case, season, audience, best-sellers) that give
   browse and campaigns a destination and improve discovery.
4. Design bundles and kits: complementary SKUs packaged at a deliberate margin to raise
   average order value without training customers to wait for discounts.
5. Set merchandising rules: sort order, featured and new-arrival placement, out-of-stock
   handling, and how hero SKUs surface across the store.
6. Instrument and iterate: product-level views, add-to-cart and conversion rate, bundle
   attach rate, and AOV by collection; promote what converts, prune what does not.

## Output

`merchandising_plan`: the catalog taxonomy, PDP standards, collection strategy, bundle
definitions, and merchandising rules, recorded in the company brain.

## Rules

- Bundles raise AOV; blanket discounts erode it. Reach for packaging and cross-sell before
  cutting price.
- Every SKU needs a clear path to discovery. A product no collection or filter surfaces is
  effectively unlisted.
- Merchandise to the data. Sort order and placement are hypotheses to test against
  conversion and attach rate, not fixed decoration.

Revisit when the catalog grows, a season turns, or a new collection or campaign launches;
deepen this plan rather than starting a separate one.
